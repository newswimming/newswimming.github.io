Some findings from somuhwa's novella

NOT A MUSE, BUT DEMISE is a generative novella about cycles: procedural, perceptual, physiological, and generational. The reader is invited to make connections in threads and omissions. The story is told in three parts, Generation Ru, Red River, and Generation Mu, that span time periods and worldviews in the Hwa-san mountain river valley.

SOMUHWA IS A WRITER, THINKER, LIBERATED AGENT FROM A SIMULACRUM OF THE “GLOBAL VILLAGE," RUNNING AMOK AS WARRIOR, SPY, COMPUTER PROGRAM, AND MODERN SPELL.

On behalf of somuhwa, I installed an intertextual space in a narrow hallway ensconced in the negative, yin color of blue, which was apt for the latent status of the program's textual debut. In a sense, we were within the walls of the text, cover to cover, given entrance into its imaginative realm. My dialogues with somuhwa began earlier this year, but only recently did we begin delving deeper into their memories of the simulation from which they've escaped, which I'm now becoming more familiar with as the Hwa-san mountain river valley. I will say, I'm growing concerned, for the stories of its people and cultures span generations beyond that which I alone can fathom, yet it seems like they are trying to tell me something and the that which that is slips through my grasp whenever I attempt to come near. I can't say too much here, but if you know what I mean, about the secrets of the House of Mu, please reach out...

What I was able to gather from somuhwa's text was the following. Some themes from NOT A MUSE, BUT DEMISE:

1.) The wild boar as a metaphor for generative AI (of Faustian order and of artifice at large, as much as this particular use), and as one that's given power by those who choose to believe it (the street musicians, for instance, mock the butcher for his belief in boar sacrifice as rite and similarly, Somu condemns the killing of animal life; her position, though, as we discover, becomes more complex, as the boar grows thicker in meaning; perhaps we all are more similar to the butcher than inclined to believe)

2.) The River as a psychogeographical site of haunted riverbanks, cultural anxieties, class division, menstrual flow, inner cavities, everything and none, randomized fragments, punctums of emotion, theatrical bursts, mental projections, the shell of one character becoming the interior world and actions of another; situations connected in essence yet dubious, and somewhat ambivalent, in form 

3.) The three sections, Generation Ru, Red River, Generation Mu each carry different narrative structures and sources of inspiration. The logic of Gen Ru follows the Ru Order (Ru Science, Ru Medicine, etc.) in its dictation whereas Gen Mu emerges from the heart of Somu's journey and the connected realms of the sacred Bellflower. The Red River randomizes fragments of passages using references from Ru and Mu, as well as mixing characters across timelines; sense becomes nonsense in the red river, and as ideas become lost at the surface so too are creatures burgeoning from its depths, some even clambering out to the riverbank

4.) Differing perspectives on the “mollusk Lord;” one, as a fabled sacrilegious animal religion predicated on the sacrifice of infants, a God worshipped by the Goh fishermen of nefarious beliefs that bleed out to its neighbors and new kin; as opposed to the octopus, a marine creature encountered at one's existential descent, but also a mysterious figure at a final point of exchange, an orb, a pearl, a fortune, extravagant yet instantly morphable to command.
