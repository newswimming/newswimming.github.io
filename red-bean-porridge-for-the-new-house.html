Red bean porridge for the new house

Traditionally, red bean porridge(팥죽) is prepared on the night of the winter solstice 동지(冬至) where the night is longest and the day shortest, making it a period charged with negative energy before the return of the sun. Because the color red is believed to embody positive 'yang' energy, red bean porridge effectively balances the 'yin' of the winter solstice. By setting a bowl of red bean porridge for ancestral spirits, at a shrine and/or around one's house, the energy of the space could be cleansed and renewed with fresh intention. Moving into a new home embodies broader life changes, a restructuring of space that inevitably also reorients one's approach to memory objects and their relation to time. You realize you can afford to make decisions based on values other than comfort or habit, and instead encourage the best vision for yourself in the architecture of a space that is co-habited with others.

As I was stirring the pot of red bean porridge, the thick, viscous red substance was entrancing and healing in many ways. It reminded me of a number of things, of refried red beans or a deliciously creamy mole a good friend once made for me in college. Making red bean porridge makes me feel that the world brewing beneath my fingertips requires careful attention. The best way to anticipate the future, I realized, is to pay respect to the present. Believing that the future exists pushes our consciousness to act accordingly, with perspective and scale, beyond our more immediate impulses. Could this be the connection between "the virtual" and virtue? Still yet, that is a question I am making the time to parse out, in an essay that's forthcoming, coming forth, to the fore, on the verge of becoming an idea always is, needing something quite more than hope and sheer will to actualize...

For now, the peace of feeling a semblance of connection is enough. When my Korean literature professor saw my short animation, Gift of Mago, she was delighted by the thick, red substance pouring onto my creature because it reminded her of red bean porridge. Now, after making my first batch, I understand why. I owe her that essay, as it's long overdue... And sadly, it is not at all about porridge but, happily, a neo-folkloric science fiction by a Korean author but sadly, so much actually a question of personal interest, I can't get myself to write...

The mythology I am constructing of the deity Mago is dense from being so absorbent. One of my friends saw the red substance and thought my creature was drowning in Pepto Bismol. The idea of a remedy for menstrual cramps is not far off from the weight of the flow itself. Menstruation and hormonal flux as the source of creative genius is the core of the divine feminine, as are, in the traditional Chinese mythology of Magu, the healing powers of the hemp leaves carried by Mago and the nourishment of the peaches she provides. Not filial piety, but generosity for an elderly woman outside her kin, who later plants the peach's seed to sprout abundance for those who walk the same path after she leaves it.

Planting seeds! But of what? It's 3AM. Better to find the answer in my dreams.

"The Unknown Cities" is based on the science fiction "9 City, Millions of Future" by Yan Wu, a story that describes the end of one era to another's speculative "return;" in this case, the end of an Anthropocentric Earth crystallizes new, even more beautiful relationships with nature across different planetary realms and possible life forms. Not unlike a little egg of sweet, glutinous rice nested within a bowl of porridge, one might hope.
