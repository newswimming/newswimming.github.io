"Shockwave Delay" by Yoshiko Chuma and the School of Hard Knocks

It takes being physically at a place to feel the weight of its past lives and the connective threads of those framing its presence. I watched Yoshiko Chuma’s haunting  performance at the LaMama theater in New York last night. Amidst the maximalist 2.5 hours of song, dance, installation after installation, video, concepts shifting and in flux, jumping from one tangent to an alternate temporality, feelings reaching approach but collapsing abruptly against external form, playing with distance, perspective, physical and imaged space— I felt seen. The beauty of it all is that it only took one moment, one brief yet intentional slip of the mouth amongst hundreds (likely thousands, ad infinitum) offered to the audience in the show; for me, it was one name that I caught– or perhaps it was the name that caught me–that warped my mind and body into the vortex of Chuma’s world. Let me tell you how it happened. It was during the second half of the show, closer to the end. Intermittently, Chuma and the Hard Knocks would turn the lights on, the audience now in view, and in essence, “pause” the show to call out names and gesture at us to participate, perhaps even inviting us to come to stage and take on a persona within the performative space. Yoshiko Chuma and Jim Fletcher would roll call a list of names to applause as part of an “Awards” or “Graduation” ceremony. In carnivalesque fashion, Chuma would yell gayly and applaud exuberantly with large clown-like balloons of bright green, red, yellow parading around in fanfare. When Chuma passed by me, she looked at me and called out Yang Song. I gasped. Yang Song was a Chinese migrant worker who fell four stories to her death after the NYPD raided her home in 2017. Like many massage parlor workers, she was unlawfully assaulted and aggressed by local authorities for no reason other than making an honest living as an Asian immigrant in the U.S. Why did she call her name, passing by me? 

Then it clicked for me that this is a show about the trauma of birth (by extension life, death), in its most immediate form, the birth of an idea, the pain of thought, of thinking itself. There was a moment near the end of the show, kind of like this approaching of chaos, with a small percussive gong (like the Korean kkwaenggwari), when all the voices in your head are spiraling into endless exhaustion, aching, speaking, desperate for relief, a breath-- before abruptly shifting to a new scene, a simple scene, of Dane Terry playing the piano under spotlight, singing what I remember as a love song. Sometimes, it takes an external view to bring you out of your own purgatory. As the song came to a close, Chuma screams “LIGHTS!!!!!” and we’re reminded where we are-- in her world-- and what else can we do but slowly, somewhat dumbfoundedly, go back home.
