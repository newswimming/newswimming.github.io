flesh of her flesh of her flesh of her flesh of her flesh of her

By newswimming Published on June 1, 2023

"it's name was Penelope" by Judy Malloy

As part of my independent study this summer I read seminal literary hypertext, "it's name was Penelope" which was created in 1990 by Judy Malloy, originally written using the BASIC program. Here is the version I read (link here) and some thoughts here for discussion with my prof tomorrow.

While the navigation of “it’s name was Penelope” allows for the taking of winding paths, the structure of the text signifies it as one with a definite start and an end. The beginning of the text starts off with ritualistic bells that announce the start of the experience, as the image of a boat “sails” forward in a linear path. Among the various hyperlinks that direct the reader’s navigation, such as “next,” “SEA,” and “song,” a hyperlink entitled “end” directly concludes the textual experience and displays the credits of the author. Moreover, the omnipresence of “end” as an option makes apparent the looming finitude of the experience throughout while one is experiencing it, much like the book as an object carries a temporality albeit its thickness, or length, in this case, is not disclosed (perhaps, though, implied after completing a section and knowing there are three more of presumably a similar length). I spent roughly two and a half hours with the text, and found its format and content to evoke much of my own everyday memories and sense of longing for the simple yet warm intimacy of loving relationships in my past. In this sense, the experience of “it’s name was Penelope” felt much like a daydream, my emotional space as a reader flowing with last weekend’s happenings and next week’s travels, distractions that detract from the textual path but remain topical in the yearning it engenders. Being in proximity with a loved one, the affect it imbues and expands with time, can alone drive the rhythm of experience that perhaps diverges into creative thinking but less so focus at one's task at hand. “There was grass growing between the grey concrete blocks on the courtyard floor,” writes Malloy, an image of stubbornly sprouting in the direction of light (and life) despite the many boundaries of adult life that contain it. Being and experience are one thing, and the distance created through time, words, language, gestures, and other communicative acts is another. I suppose the balance of work and life is somewhere in between. 

By maintaining a semblance of continuity by having a start and end, Malloy marks the text as a modernist one that privileges the reader as a cohesive conscious unit but she also rewrites an essence to modernity by inviting interactivity via hypertext. In doing so, Malloy equips digital techniques to invoke a more traditional format of narrative – oral storytelling– which, like interactive digital text, is in essence about dialogue rather than a singular story.  While the Muse tells the story, she advises the reader to do so “beginning where you will,” inviting interactivity that parallels how oral storytelling traditionally operates. For instance, oral storytelling is grounded in a relational dynamic between the storyteller and the audience, questions and answers that reformulate the content that is exchanged between the two. In this way the fixity of having a start and end is somewhat in tension with the interactivity of hypertext, but I argue figures the complexity of Malloy’s reworking of narrative structure. 

Incorporating mythology as a narrative framework is a performative strategy of the digital format. The start of the text employs the boat and its associated mythology, via excerpts from The Odyssey, as the primary metaphor for navigation, but as we progress through the textual experience, the boat then becomes a toy boat that the narrator is taking photographs of, her photographic framework taking precedence to perhaps the romanticism of the originating journey. In the section “Fine Work and Wide Across,” for instance, Malloy writes, “I was sorting through photographs, looking for an image of the toy boat” and the book being read instead is “STRUCTURAL(ISM) AND PHOTOGRAPHY by Lew Thomas." In a sense, the text follows an evolution from the romanticism of childhood and the luxury of time in piecing together “handspun threads” (as was written of the weaving metaphor in one of the Song sections, quoting The Odyssey”) into a more more structurally driven practice of photography and its codification of moments into a technological apparatus (“I began to rearrange the photos in my mind”) which evoke organization in atomized pieces rather than a woven, interconnected whole. The photographic images are a construction (“Through the lens of my camera, the beer cans and discarded contraceptives which littered the shore were not visible”) and loss of innocence of experience that once was pure. In this particular passage, the inability for the narrator to keep up with the idealism of childhood is made clear:

“The little boat veered from the shore
and began to sail out into the middle of the pond.
I took off my sneakers, rolled up my pants,
and waded in after it with my camera in my hands.
I moved gingerly because although the bottom
was soft and slimy in some places,
in other places it was covered with sharp little stones.
The boat was moving faster than I was,
and it stayed several feet ahead of me.”

The fixity of having a start and end as a structural arc does not necessarily follow that of birth and death per se, but another temporal pattern that involves a relationship between childhood and adult life, idealism and depression, dreams and reality. Unlike the other sections that can be revisited, the first section, “dawn,” of childhood memory, only occurs once, it is more difficult to access, thus requires the reader also to recollect their memory during the rest of the textual experience. The rest of the sections, on the other hand, can be revisited, fragments of adult life operating on somewhat repetitive cycles or patterns of thought. Our waking moments are in some sense packaged into atomized sequences of memory, recursively following certain psycho-emotional patterns stemming from childhood. The third section, “Rock and a hard place,” captures the narrator’s depression when her loved one leaves her, evoking the loss and lack felt through unrequited love, the end of a relationship, being swamped with work via the computer, distance felt through the office objects repeatedly scattered throughout the text. Around this time, presumably might be when a reader recognizes it is a suitable time to “Stop,” or perhaps they might return to earlier memories of kittens chasing grasshoppers or stories of childhood on long drives. In my own reading of this text, I somehow missed the “dawn” section all together at the beginning. I must have immediately clicked SEA after the first block of text from dawn without knowing. I read the About page after going through all the sections and had to start over to read the rest of dawn. You can imagine my surprise when I realized it was the toy boat whose name was Penelope all along. Like discovering an old memory of my own, the text was given a charming new meaning, a sense that everything was meant to be the way it was all along.
