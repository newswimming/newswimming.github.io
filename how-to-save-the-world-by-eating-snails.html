How to Save the World by Eating Snails

As part of our fellowship with the Arts + Climate collective at USC, Ellie and I made a trip to Santa Barbara to become more familiar with its shellfish industry and try out some local flavors for a dinner party series we are curating. Shellfish is one of the most sustainable seafoods to eat in California, especially those caught and cultured by local fisheries and aquaculture farms. Most seafood in California though is imported from Asia with quite wide-ranging fishing regulations and marine conservation practices. It's wild to think transcontinental import is so much cheaper than purchasing locally, and part of this research, for us, has to do with better understanding the design of food systems, especially with emerging methods like aquaculture in the market.

On our walk on the beach, we encountered a dolphin washed up onto shore. Its stomach was ripped open with its guts spilling out, which I haven't pictured here; I wondered what happened, how it got there, and felt strangely fixated on confronting the fleshiness of its inner parts. The precarity of the dolphin's habitat lingered as an omen on our trip.

In Santa Barbara, we were most interested in sea urchin, which divers harvest in local waters, black mussels, which is cultured in open water farms in ecologically beneficial ways (for plankton regulation, for instance), and abalone, which is now outlawed for harvesting, but found on an impressive aquaculture farm out here. When we visited the Santa Barbara fish market, we focused on the sounds. Our audio piece has to do with navigating peripheral ambiance. Somewhat auto-ethnographic, in being visitors ourselves, we paid attention to the borders of cultural perception- the squeamish child and her Japanese American mother, the excitement of liveness beside a lobster's tank. How does a fascination in an exotic animal contribute to its cultural capital, the acceptability of its consumption, a drive for its purchase? I was personally most fascinated by the purple hotchi sea urchin given my own cultural background in which uni is a prized delicacy as sashimi. Since a decade ago upwards to 80% of the urchin harvested in Santa Barbara was exported to Japan. I noticed local Santa Barbara folks, like the guy working at a fishing shop, have never tried it, nor abalone, before; it's more of a tourist attraction than a part of the local taste, I suppose.

Ellie and I wanted to snorkel, but a terrible spillage of over 1 million gallons of untreated sewage was polluting Goleta Beach. We were advised not to snorkel today. We looked for sea slugs and found an octopus at the tidepools instead.

Currently, shellfish and kelp aquaculture farming face some regulatory challenges in California. But given the fact of the ocean becoming more acidic due to climate change, affecting the calcium carbonate levels shellfish need to grow, and of mercury pollution in tuna amongst other toxins impacting the health of larger mammals like the dolphin, I wonder what the future of aquaculture could hold. How can we mobilize the cultural imagination to cultivate more sustainable, local foods? Talking to a food justice researcher at USC, at least in Los Angeles' case, we learn that the part of the effort begins, at a basic level, with promoting seafood accessibility all together, promoting the idea that seafood can be fresh and delicious, not frozen and nauseating. At this stage of our research, it's hard to say what's to be done or how to bridge that gap.

It's a strange idea. The mythologies surrounding food dishes being so disconnected from the actual natural ecosystems (or industrial systems) they are situated in. I get it... I remember borrowing my friend's car to drive thirty minutes in North Carolina to an H-Mart, where I'd buy eel or kimchi or whatever have you that reminded me of my culture. Supermarkets are somewhat of a postmodern emporium in that way, a global network of relations that maintains the guise of option and accessibility at the cost of occluding the realities of its origins, its labor processes, its environmental impacts. What of these attachments to "culture," though? To what degree are they manufactured by media, and precisely that-- a myth? To that end, I am a proponent of the evolution of culture and being present to placemaking, as Angelenos, as much as to memory, as diaspora. At the end of the day, we are imbricated in very material realities within the natural world and far past due paying attention to it… maybe that could give us a clue on the urgency of change, the need for ritual expulsions of attachment, and how to form new cultures and communities for understanding meaning within our lives, as uncomfortable, awkward, maybe painful as that process could be...

On the production of the Zero Waste (2023) documentary last year, participating in a beach clean-up gave a sense of scale seeing all sorts of things wash up upon shore: large, knotted ropes from industrial ships, blocks of styrofoam smoothly sculpted like a pebble, and bottles. A whole lot of bottles. Plastic bottles coming from everywhere. So maybe sustainability, then, has less to do about the anxieties of the single consumer, and more to do about understanding global scale, global industrial scale, which has most to do with poverty, not the environment, though of course the two are always so deeply related, which leaves me somewhere but nowhere at the same time.

What of my relationship with the ocean? Relaxing has been more of my issue and giving myself artistic liberty to be present to my surroundings. Perhaps these relationships first start with just dipping your toes; thinking about them, writing about them, doing whatever it takes to give your mind space to just be. Spending time with Ellie, whose practice as a marine photographer, a swimmer, a diver, and ecologist in many ways, her gentle way of being, offered place to start.

Last night was a full moon, and we sat at the edge of the pier with urchin, scallop, mussels, and calamari on our plates. We discussed our families, hopes and motivations, the persistence of rage, and grounding force of gratitude. The backdrop was romantic and deep, indigo blue, while the conversation was casual, every day. I am told to avoid most shrimp, which is a disappointment, but negotiable, and evolving, improving, with the health of Vietnam's mangroves. Salmon, tilapia, a lot of these other finfishes, too, she says. With shellfish, though, I'm content, to have at least that, for now, that is.
